GPU-Driven Procedural Marine Scene

A real-time marine environment developed in Unity using geometry, compute and tessellation shaders to generate animated seagulls, fish schools and a dynamic ocean surface.

Technologies Unity, C#, HLSL and Universal Render Pipeline
Techniques Geometry shaders, compute shaders, tessellation and displacement
Project type Collaborative university project

Project overview

The objective of this project was to create a dynamic marine scene containing animated seagulls, schools of fish and a procedurally displaced ocean.

Geometry shaders generate the visible creatures from point clouds, while compute shaders update their movement on the GPU. The sea uses tessellation, displacement mapping, adaptive detail and a Fresnel-based water appearance.

Real-time demonstration of the marine scene, including flock movement, animated waves, wireframe visualization and adaptive tessellation.

1. Procedural creature geometry

The fish and seagulls are generated from point-based input geometry. Geometry shaders expand each point into a set of triangles that form the visible shape of each creature.

Time-dependent sine functions animate the seagulls’ wings and the fishes’ tails, creating local deformation directly on the GPU.

GEOMETRY SHADER 01

Seagull generation

Each input point is expanded into procedural geometry representing a seagull. Wing vertices are displaced over time to simulate flapping.

GEOMETRY SHADER 02

Fish generation

Fish bodies and tails are created procedurally. The tail geometry oscillates through a time-dependent deformation.

2. GPU flock simulation

Separate compute shaders update the position and direction of the fish and seagulls in parallel.

Their movement is based on the three principal Boids rules: alignment, cohesion and separation. These behaviours allow the creatures to move as coordinated groups rather than as isolated objects.

RULE 01

Alignment

Each creature adjusts its direction according to nearby members of the group.

RULE 02

Cohesion

Individuals move toward the local centre of the flock or school.

RULE 03

Separation

A repulsion force prevents neighbouring creatures from overlapping.

3. Ocean tessellation and displacement

The ocean surface is generated through a tessellation shader. The hull stage controls mesh subdivision, while the domain stage displaces the generated vertices according to a height map.

A time-dependent trigonometric function animates the displaced vertices, producing a moving wave surface.

TESSELLATION

Hull and domain stages

The hull shader defines the subdivision factor, and the domain shader calculates the final displaced position of each generated vertex.

DISPLACEMENT

Animated waves

Height-map data and time-based movement deform the ocean mesh, producing a dynamic surface.

4. Adaptive tessellation

The tessellation level changes according to the distance between the ocean surface and the camera.

Nearby regions receive more geometric detail, while distant areas use fewer triangles. This provides a level-of-detail effect without tessellating the entire ocean at maximum density.

5. Wireframe and Fresnel water

A wireframe visualization can be enabled to inspect the generated triangle density and confirm the effect of adaptive tessellation.

The water appearance also includes a Fresnel term. The sea becomes more reflective near the horizon and more transparent when viewed from above.

6. Camera interaction

The camera can move freely through the scene using keyboard and mouse controls.

MOVEMENT

Keyboard controls

WASD controls horizontal movement, while Q and E move the camera vertically.

ROTATION

Mouse look

Mouse movement controls camera orientation, allowing the scene to be inspected from different distances and viewing angles.
